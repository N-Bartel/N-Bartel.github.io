PCB Design and Manufacter

Nicholas is currently a member of a micro gravity fuel gauging experiment called Modal Propellant Gauging. In the winter of 2018 Modal Propellant Gauging was sending a payload aboard a Blue Origin rocket. The team was experiencing trouble triggering the data recording system upon launch due to our unconventional DAQ. After some phone calls with the manufacturer of the DAQ, they recommended we build a circuit to trigger the data taking process. The first step was to turn the pen and paper design into a schematic diagram on the computer. The EDA, Electronic Design Automation, software Nicholas chose for this task was KiCAD due to it being open source and easy to work with. After Nicholas created the schematic diagram on KiCAD, the schematic must be transferred over to KiCAD's PCB maker. In the PCB maker Nicholas drew in the traces which would later be used to carry current in the final product. In the third image you can see a 3D rendering of what the final product will look like. Finally, Nicholas used a PCB, Printed Circuit Board, mill to cut out the copper board as designed. Nicholas had to learn the mill's software called Otherplan in order to have the board cut. After the copper board was cut, the surface mount electronics had to be mounted. Conventionally, these parts would be soldered on by hand but with some of the capacitors being about 1/32nd of an inch in size they had to be attached a different way. This was accomplished by baking the parts onto the board. A liquid solder is applied to the piece using a syringe and then the board is "baked" in an infrared oven until the solder hardens.
